// about.me

The Engineer Behind the Work

My first real jump into production systems was a Tier 2 Application Support role at a major energy systems operator. I'd done the prep work — certifications, some community college networking and security — but I'd never supported a live production system, never shipped an app. Someone before me had tried the same jump and it hadn't worked out. I don't know what happened with him. I know what it felt like for me, walking in every morning: lack of confidence, fear of the unknown, real imposter syndrome.

I'm also Black, in a field where I don't see a lot of people who look like me doing what I'm trying to do — building infrastructure, teaching systems thinking, operating at this level. That's not a footnote. It's part of why I pay close attention to what actually works when someone's starting from "I don't know if I belong here," because I've started from there myself, more than once.

What got me through wasn't knowing more than the people around me. It was a habit I didn't have a name for yet: instead of memorizing the steps in front of me, I'd ask what the system was actually doing — what depended on what, what would break if this piece changed, what the previous engineer was protecting against when they wrote it this way. A ticket isn't just a ticket. It's a symptom of a system with a shape. Once you can see the shape, the specific tool in front of you stops being the scary part — because the shape repeats. Every distributed system has dependencies. Every release has a blast radius. Every config has a reason someone made it that way, even if that reason is undocumented.

It happened again years later, at a fast-moving automotive tech company building cloud-native infrastructure. Different tools, different documentation conventions, different language for the same underlying ideas — but the same shape underneath. I wasn't fluent in their stack on day one. I was fluent in the pattern. That's what let me catch up fast instead of drowning.

That's the actual thing I teach. Not a confidence trick. Not "believe in yourself." Technical skill, real and specific — Linux, networking, virtualization, observability — taught alongside the pattern recognition that makes the skill portable. Today's tool will be replaced. The shape underneath it — dependency, blast radius, root cause, recovery — won't. Design with that shape in mind, and you're not just fixing today's ticket. You're building something your future self, and the next engineer who inherits it, will thank you for.
